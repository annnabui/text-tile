CHAPTER 3
EXPERIMENT 1

SELF-GENERATION IN MULTIPLICITIES


It is crucial to remember that this project began from observing tiles as a feature of post-French colonial architecture throughout Vietnam, hence, an investigation into encaustic tile isn’t complete until it is considered in its environmental context––they’re not just decorative elements but active participants in the built environment, engaging with the people who move across them. Material-wise, the porous nature of cement traps air, making encaustic tiles surprisingly vocal and playful underfoot.

Hence, the final experiment manifests as a self-generating set of tile patterns that dynamically respond to their environment, symbolizing the reclamation and revitalization of these designs. This adaptability ensures their continued relevance, even amidst shifting political landscapes.

I made a floor TouchDesigner projection, inspired by elekktronaut’s tutorial on YouTube, to generate tile patterns using only two shapes––a square and a circle––with the patterns responsive to the sound of external footsteps.17

I used two layers: a 2×2 grid of circles (to highlight fourfold symmetry) and a 45°-rotated square (to simulate diagonal bilateral symmetry), controlling the blending modes between these layers to create different patterns.17

There are 46 blending modes in TouchDesigner, but realistically, I shouldn’t be expecting 46 different outcomes since a few of these blending modes return similar results. I connected the blending-mode cycling mechanism in my TouchDesigner file to my laptop’s microphone, allowing the cycles to respond dynamically to environmental sound amplitudes. This way, the visuals echo the sensory experience of walking on these tiles in real life.

Although previous mini-experiments were done in p5js, I figured TouchDesigner was more convenient for a generative project. If this was to live on p5js, I would have had to use a much stronger computer than my Macbook Air M1 for the program to run motion, much less computing generative patterns.
